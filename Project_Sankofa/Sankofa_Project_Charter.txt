1. Executive Summary
An AI Academic Survival Copilot built for students navigating one of the most under-addressed realities in African higher education: incomplete curriculum delivery. Across federal universities, lecturers routinely fail to cover full semester syllabi, teaching is often rushed or generalized, and exam preparation becomes a guessing game rather than a structured process. It closes this gap directly.
Rather than assuming a complete, well-structured educational pipeline — the assumption baked into most global EdTech and AI study tools — this tool is designed for fragmentation. Students upload whatever they actually have: scanned notes, lecturer PDFs, WhatsApp screenshots, past questions, and voice recordings. The system reconstructs a coherent learning pathway from these fragments, identifies what has not been taught, and estimates what is likely to appear on the exam.
The product is anchored by three hero capabilities: the Curriculum Gap Detector, the Exam Probability Engine, and the AI Survival Tutor, supported by a standout urgency feature, Panic Mode, for 48-hour exam crunches. This charter defines the strategic rationale, technical architecture, and 3-day delivery plan for building a working, judge-ready MVP that demonstrates category-defining innovation while remaining realistic to execute under hackathon constraints.
It does not teach a complete curriculum. It rebuilds the one the student actually received — and tells them exactly what to do about the rest.
2. Problem Statement
Nigerian and African university students face a structural, systemic learning gap that has nothing to do with their own effort or ability.
The Core Problem
* Incomplete curriculum delivery: Lecturers frequently do not finish the semester's official course outline due to time constraints, strikes, administrative disruption, or scheduling gaps.
* Inconsistent teaching quality: Even delivered content is often rushed, generalized, or pitched at a pace that assumes strong prior preparation, leaving slower learners behind.
* Fragmented learning materials: Students accumulate a patchwork of scanned handouts, PDFs, WhatsApp forwards, personal notes, and past questions with no unifying structure.
* Opaque exam expectations: Students rarely know which topics are likely to be tested, forcing broad, inefficient revision instead of targeted preparation.
* Infrastructure constraints: Poor and expensive internet access and unreliable electricity limit how much data-heavy or session-dependent studying is realistic.
The compounding effect is educational inequality: a student's exam outcome depends less on their diligence and more on which lecturer they were assigned and how much of the syllabus that lecturer happened to complete. This is the problem we're building to solve.
3. Market Context (Africa-Specific)
Nigeria's tertiary education system serves millions of students across federal, state, and private universities, with federal institutions representing a dense, underserved market for tools built around local realities rather than imported assumptions.
Structural Realities Shaping the Market
* Frequent disruptions to the academic calendar (strikes, resource shortages) routinely compress teaching time within a semester.
* Large lecture-hall class sizes limit individualized attention and personalized pacing.
* Mobile-first, data-conscious student behavior: most students access the internet primarily through smartphones on limited data bundles.
* A strong existing culture of informal knowledge-sharing — WhatsApp study groups, shared PDFs, “spot the question” past-question culture — that it can formalize rather than replace.
* Low penetration of AI-native study tools purpose-built for the African academic calendar and grading culture, versus high awareness of global tools like ChatGPT, which are used informally but not structured around curriculum gaps.
Why Now
Smartphone penetration and generative AI awareness among Nigerian undergraduates have both risen sharply, while no dominant local product has yet claimed the “exam survival” category. Global players are not built for fragmented, disrupted curricula — they assume the curriculum was actually taught. This is an open strategic gap.
4. Vision Statement
A future where no African student's academic success is determined by how much of the syllabus their lecturer managed to teach.
This tool envisions a generation of students equipped with an AI copilot that adapts to the real, imperfect conditions of their education — turning fragmented, incomplete learning experiences into structured, confident exam readiness, regardless of institutional or infrastructural limitations.
5. Mission Statement
To give every student the tools to reconstruct their own complete education from whatever fragments they have — and to walk into every exam knowing exactly what matters most.
The mission is executed through three commitments: meeting students where their materials actually are (not where an idealized curriculum assumes they should be), translating fragmented content into a clear map of covered and missing knowledge, and converting that map into a prioritized, time-boxed action plan students can trust under real academic pressure.
6. Product Overview
This is a web-based AI platform where students upload the fragmented materials available to them for a course — lecturer slides or PDFs, personal notes, scanned handwriting, past questions, and (optionally) voice recordings — and receive three coordinated outputs: a diagnostic map of curriculum coverage, a probability-ranked list of likely exam topics, and a conversational AI tutor grounded specifically in the student's own uploaded materials via retrieval-augmented generation (RAG).
It is explicitly positioned as an academic survival tool, not a general-purpose study assistant or a note-organizing app. Every feature is designed around a single question: given incomplete teaching, what does this student need to do right now to be ready for the exam?

	12. Detailed Feature Breakdown
12.1 Hero Feature 1 — Curriculum Gap Detector
The system ingests the official course outline alongside all uploaded materials, then uses semantic comparison to determine which outline topics are substantively represented in the materials versus absent or only superficially mentioned.
* Input: Course outline (text or PDF) + all uploaded lecture materials for that course.
* Process: Topic extraction from the outline, semantic matching against embedded material chunks, coverage scoring per topic.
* Output: Overall coverage percentage (e.g., “Covered: 62%”) and a ranked list of likely-missing high-priority topics (e.g., Trees, Graph Traversal, Dynamic Programming).
This is its core innovation and primary differentiator — no mainstream EdTech or AI study tool performs outline-versus-materials gap analysis for the African context.
12.2 Hero Feature 2 — Exam Probability Engine
Using uploaded past questions, topic frequency across those questions, and emphasis signals in lecture materials (repetition, worked examples, highlighted terms), the system estimates the relative likelihood that each topic appears on the upcoming exam.
* Input: Past questions, lecture materials, gap-detection output.
* Process: Frequency analysis of topics across past questions combined with emphasis weighting from materials.
* Output: A ranked “High Probability Topics” list with percentage estimates (e.g., Deadlocks 82%, CPU Scheduling 75%).
* Critical framing: All outputs must be clearly labeled as probability estimates based on historical patterns — never presented as guaranteed exam predictions.
12.3 Hero Feature 3 — AI Survival Tutor
A conversational RAG-powered tutor grounded entirely in the student's own uploaded materials, so answers reflect what was actually taught rather than a generic external syllabus.
* Sample interactions: “Explain recursion,” “What topics matter most?” “What am I missing?” “Help me revise in 2 days.”
* Tone: Simple language, local analogies, undergraduate-friendly examples.
* Future language support: Nigerian Pidgin, Yoruba, Hausa, and Igbo, to widen accessibility beyond English-only instruction.
12.4 Standout Feature — Panic Mode
A single high-visibility action — “EXAM IN 48 HOURS” — that reorients the entire platform around urgent, triaged revision.
* Generates a prioritized topic list weighted toward high-probability, low-coverage areas.
* Builds a rapid, time-boxed revision schedule across the remaining hours.
* Surfaces likely questions drawn from the Exam Probability Engine.
* Produces cram-focused quizzes targeting the highest-priority gaps first.
Panic Mode is the emotional and demo centerpiece of the product — it converts anxiety into a concrete plan within seconds, and it demonstrates all three hero features working together in a single, visceral moment.
12.5 Deprioritized Features (Post-MVP)
* Quiz generation beyond Panic Mode cram quizzes (stretch goal)
* Slide and podcast/video generation
* Study Squads (3–8 student accountability groups) and social learning spaces
* Leaderboards, push notifications, and native mobile app
These were deliberately cut from hackathon scope — see Section 13 for full MVP rationale.
13. MVP Scope
With only 3 days of build time, Our MVP is deliberately narrow and depth-focused rather than broad and shallow. The goal is a convincing, working prototype that proves the core innovation — not a feature-complete startup.
Must-Have (In Scope)
* 1. Course Material Upload & Parsing — PDF text extraction and OCR for scanned/handwritten notes, chunked and embedded into a vector store.
* 2. Curriculum Gap Detector — Outline-vs-materials comparison producing a coverage percentage and missing-topics list.
* 3. AI Study Copilot (RAG Chat) — Conversational tutor grounded in the student's own uploaded materials.
* 4. Exam Panic Mode — One-click 48-hour revision plan combining gap data and topic prioritization.
Optional (If Time Permits)
* Cram-focused quiz generation within Panic Mode
* A simple dashboard visualizing coverage percentage and priority topics
Explicitly Out of Scope
* Full social networking features (Study Squads, leaderboards, shared groups)
* Podcast or video generation
* Push notifications and study reminders
* Native mobile application
This scope was chosen because it isolates and proves the three hero differentiators end-to-end, without diluting build time on generic features that any note-taking app could offer.

15. AI System Design
15.1 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) Core
All AI outputs — tutor answers, gap detection, and panic mode plans — are grounded in the student's own uploaded materials via RAG, rather than relying on the LLM's general knowledge alone. This ensures answers reflect what was actually taught in the student's specific class, not a generic external syllabus.
15.2 Curriculum Gap Detection Logic
* Extract discrete topics from the official course outline.
* Embed each outline topic and compare against embedded material chunks using semantic similarity.
* Assign a coverage score per topic; topics below a defined similarity threshold are flagged as likely-missing.
* Aggregate topic scores into an overall coverage percentage.
15.3 Exam Probability Scoring Logic
* Extract topics referenced in uploaded past questions and tally frequency across available years/sets.
* Weight topics by frequency plus emphasis signals in materials (repetition, worked examples).
* Normalize into a relative probability ranking, explicitly labeled as an estimate.
15.4 Prompt & Tone Design
* Tutor prompts instruct the model to prioritize plain language, local analogies, and undergraduate-level explanations.
* Panic Mode prompts instruct the model to be directive and prioritized, not exploratory — urgency-appropriate tone.
* All probability-related outputs are prompt-constrained to include explicit “estimate, not guarantee” framing.
16. Data Flow Architecture
Data pipeline follows a linear ingestion-to-insight flow:
* 1. Upload Files → Student uploads PDFs, notes, or scanned images for a specific course.
* 2. Text Extraction → PyPDF/pdfplumber extracts text from digital documents; Tesseract OCR handles scanned/handwritten content.
* 3. Chunking → Extracted text is split into semantically coherent chunks sized for embedding and retrieval.
* 4. Embeddings → Each chunk is converted into a vector representation.
* 5. Vector Database → Embeddings are stored in ChromaDB under a per-course namespace.
* 6. RAG Engine → User queries or system tasks (gap detection, panic mode) retrieve the most relevant chunks.
* 7. AI Outputs → Retrieved context is passed to the LLM to generate tutor answers, gap reports, probability rankings, or panic-mode plans.
17. User Journey
Journey: Ada Prepares for Her Data Structures Exam
* Step 1 — Onboarding: Ada creates an account and sets up her “Data Structures” course.
* Step 2 — Upload: She uploads her lecturer's PDF slides, her handwritten notes (photographed), and two years of past questions.
* Step 3 — Gap Detection: The tool processes the materials and reports 62% curriculum coverage, flagging Trees, Graph Traversal, and Dynamic Programming as likely-missing.
* Step 4 — Tutor Interaction: Ada asks the AI Survival Tutor to “explain graph traversal like I'm hearing it for the first time,” and receives a plain-language explanation with a local analogy.
* Step 5 — Exam Approaches: With two days left, Ada activates Panic Mode.
* Step 6 — Panic Mode Output: She receives a prioritized 48-hour schedule, a likely-questions list, and cram quizzes targeting her weakest, highest-probability topics.
* Step 7 — Walks into the Exam: Ada enters the exam hall having spent her limited time on exactly the material most likely to matter.
18. UI/UX Principles
* Clarity over decoration: Every screen answers one question at a glance — what's covered, what's missing, what to do next.
* Urgency-aware design: Panic Mode uses distinct visual treatment (bold color, minimal navigation) to signal a shift into crisis mode.
* Low-bandwidth first: Minimal imagery, lightweight assets, and fast load times respect real data constraints.
* Mobile-responsive by default: Most students will access it primarily via smartphone browsers.
* Trustworthy probability framing: Probability figures are always shown with clear “estimate” labeling and supporting rationale, never as bare, unexplained numbers.
* Encouraging, non-judgmental tone: Copy and tutor responses avoid shaming language around gaps or missed material — the tone is supportive, not punitive.

	20. Market Differentiation
Its differentiation is not incremental — it is categorical. Most competitors optimize for organizing or summarizing content that already exists in complete form. It is built for the opposite condition: incomplete, fragmented, and inconsistent source material, which is the actual lived reality for African university students.
* Not “African NotebookLM”: It does not merely summarize what's uploaded — it diagnoses what's missing relative to an official standard (the course outline) and prescribes action.
* Diagnostic, not just generative: The Curriculum Gap Detector and Exam Probability Engine produce structured, decision-relevant intelligence, not just generated content.
* Urgency-native: Panic Mode is purpose-built for the exam-driven survival mode that characterizes real African student behavior — not an afterthought feature.
* Infrastructure-aware: Designed from the ground up for data costs and connectivity limits, not retrofitted after a data-heavy global product failed to gain local traction.

25. Demo Strategy
The demo must land the core insight within the first minute: this is not another AI note tool, it is a diagnostic system for incomplete education.
Recommended Demo Flow
* Open with the problem: State plainly that most Nigerian university lecturers do not finish the syllabus — and that this is normalized, unspoken, and educationally devastating.
* Show the upload: Upload a real (or realistic sample) set of fragmented materials for a course live on stage.
* Reveal the gap: Show the Curriculum Gap Detector output — “Covered: 62%” — and let the missing-topics list land as the emotional turning point of the demo.
* Demonstrate the tutor: Ask the AI Survival Tutor a real question live, showing grounded, locally-toned explanation.
* Close with Panic Mode: Trigger “EXAM IN 48 HOURS” live and show the generated prioritized plan — this is the memorable closing beat.
Judging Q&A Preparation
* Be ready to explain the difference between the Exam Probability Engine and simple keyword frequency counting.
* Be ready to address OCR accuracy limitations honestly, with a clear improvement roadmap.
* Be ready to explain why probability framing (not “prediction”) was a deliberate ethical design choice.

	28. Scalability Strategy
* Technical scalability: Per-course vector namespaces in ChromaDB (or a migration to a managed vector DB) support horizontal growth across institutions without cross-contamination of retrieval.
* Content scalability: As more students upload materials for the same course, the Curriculum Gap Detector and Exam Probability Engine become more accurate through aggregated (anonymized) signal — a natural network effect.
* Geographic scalability: The core architecture is course-outline-agnostic; expansion to new universities requires only new outline ingestion, not architectural change.
* Language scalability: The RAG and prompt layer is designed to extend to additional local languages without re-architecting the retrieval pipeline.
29. Closing Strategic Statement
This tool is not competing to be a better study app. It is solving a problem no one else has named directly: African students are being examined on curricula they were never fully taught, and no tool has been built to close that specific gap.
The three-day hackathon build is designed to prove one thing convincingly: that an AI system grounded in a student's actual, fragmented materials can diagnose what's missing and prescribe exactly what to do about it — under real time pressure, with real data constraints, for real students at institutions like UNN.
Every scoping decision in this charter — from the deprioritization of social features to the strict MVP boundary — exists to protect that single, demonstrable proof point. Its long-term opportunity is significant, but its immediate mandate is narrow and clear: build the Curriculum Gap Detector, the Exam Probability Engine, the AI Survival Tutor, and Panic Mode, and prove that incomplete education does not have to mean incomplete exam readiness.
